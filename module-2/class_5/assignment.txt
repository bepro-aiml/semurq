### Task 3: Descriptive Statistics — With Meaning
**What to do:** Calculate mean, variance, and standard deviation manually for a sample of 10 values, verify with NumPy, and interpret each measure.

**How to do it:**

- Step 1: Pick one numerical column (e.g., Sales) and extract a sample of 10 values:
  ```python
  import pandas as pd
  import numpy as np

  df = pd.read_csv('superstore_cleaned.csv')
  sample = df['Sales'].sample(10, random_state=42).values
  print("Sample values:", sample)
  ```

- Step 2: Calculate mean manually. Mean = sum of values / count:
  ```python
  # Manual calculation
  manual_mean = sum(sample) / len(sample)
  print(f"Manual mean: {manual_mean:.2f}")
  ```

- Step 3: Calculate variance manually. Variance = average of squared differences from mean:
  ```python
  # Manual calculation
  squared_diffs = [(x - manual_mean)**2 for x in sample]
  manual_variance = sum(squared_diffs) / len(sample)
  print(f"Manual variance: {manual_variance:.2f}")
  ```

- Step 4: Calculate standard deviation. StdDev = square root of variance:
  ```python
  manual_std = manual_variance ** 0.5
  print(f"Manual std dev: {manual_std:.2f}")
  ```

- Step 5: Verify with NumPy:
  ```python
  print(f"NumPy mean: {np.mean(sample):.2f}")
  print(f"NumPy variance: {np.var(sample):.2f}")
  print(f"NumPy std dev: {np.std(sample):.2f}")
  ```

- Step 6: In a text cell, interpret each measure:
  - What does the mean tell you? (The central/typical value of sales)
  - What does variance tell you that mean does not? (How spread out the values are)
  - If standard deviation is large relative to the mean, what does that imply? (High variability — the "typical" value is not very typical)

**Tool:** `np.mean()`, `np.var()`, `np.std()` — NumPy statistical functions

### Task 4: Correlation Analysis
**What to do:** Compute correlations between Sales, Profit, and Discount. Interpret the results and think about implications for modeling.

**How to do it:**

- Step 1: Compute individual correlations:
  ```python
  corr_sales_profit = df['Sales'].corr(df['Profit'])
  corr_sales_discount = df['Sales'].corr(df['Discount'])
  print(f"Sales-Profit correlation: {corr_sales_profit:.4f}")
  print(f"Sales-Discount correlation: {corr_sales_discount:.4f}")
  ```

- Step 2: Generate the full correlation matrix:
  ```python
  corr_matrix = df[['Sales', 'Profit', 'Discount']].corr()
  print(corr_matrix)
  ```

- Step 3: In a text cell, interpret the results:
  - Which pair is most strongly correlated?
  - Is any relationship negative? What does a negative correlation mean in this context?
  - If two features have a correlation of 0.95, should you include both in a model? Why or why not? (Hint: think about multicollinearity — redundant information)
- Step 4: Optional bonus — visualize with a scatter plot:
  ```python
  import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
  plt.figure(figsize=(8, 5))
  plt.scatter(df['Sales'], df['Profit'], alpha=0.3, s=10)
  plt.title('Sales vs Profit')
  plt.xlabel('Sales ($)')
  plt.ylabel('Profit ($)')
  plt.show()
  ```

**Tool:** `df.corr()` — computes pairwise Pearson correlation, `df['col1'].corr(df['col2'])` for individual pairs

## Tips
- Variance and standard deviation are about spread. Two datasets can have the same mean but wildly different variance. That difference matters for ML — high-variance features dominate distance calculations.
- A high positive correlation means features move together. Negative means they move in opposite directions. Near zero means no linear relationship.
- When comparing your manual calculations to NumPy, small floating-point differences are normal. As long as they match to 2 decimal places, you are fine.
