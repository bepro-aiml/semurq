**Related slides:** Class 2 — Foundations: Probability, Statistics, ML Workflow
**Estimated time:** 30 minutes

## Objective
Understand how probability underpins real-world predictions and why uncertainty is a fundamental part of machine learning.

## What You Need
- Tool: Google Docs or any word processor
- No code required
- Reference: Class 2 notes on probability, distributions, and the ML workflow

## Tasks

### Task 1: Find 3 Real-World Probability Examples
**What to do:** Identify 3 real-world situations where probability is used to make predictions.

**How to do it:**

- Step 1: Think of domains where predictions are made under uncertainty: weather, medicine, email filtering, insurance, recommendations, sports betting.
- Step 2: For each example, write 2-3 sentences answering: What is being predicted? What data might be used? Why is 100% certainty impossible?
- Step 3: Use concrete numbers where possible. For example: "Weather forecasts are roughly 80% accurate for 24-hour predictions" is stronger than "weather forecasts are sometimes wrong."

**Tool:** Google Docs

**Help if stuck:** StatQuest — Probability Fundamentals (21 min): https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pYxNSUDSFH4

**Slide reference:** This was covered in Class 2, Slide: "Probability — How Likely Is It?"

### Task 2: Why ML Models Can Never Be 100% Accurate
**What to do:** Write 1 paragraph (5-8 sentences) explaining why perfect accuracy is impossible in ML. Reference at least one concept from class.

**How to do it:**

- Step 1: Review these concepts from the lecture: noise in data, bias-variance tradeoff, data quality issues, distribution shift.
- Step 2: Pick at least one of these concepts and explain how it creates inherent uncertainty.
- Step 3: Give a concrete example. For instance: "A spam filter trained on 2023 emails may miss new spam tactics in 2024 because the distribution has shifted."
- Step 4: Write clearly. Avoid vague statements like "because data is imperfect." Explain specifically what kind of imperfection and why it matters.

**Tool:** Same Google Doc

**Help if stuck:** 3Blue1Brown — Bayes' Theorem (17 min, explains uncertainty in predictions): https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HZGCoVF3YvM

**Slide reference:** This was covered in Class 2, Slides: "Bias-Variance Tradeoff" and "The ML Workflow"

## Tips
- Think about what makes each problem inherently uncertain — is it noisy data, incomplete information, or changing conditions?
- Keep it simple. Clear thinking beats fancy vocabulary.
- The goal is to show you understand why uncertainty exists, not to list technical terms.
